# Dutch East India Company (VOC)

Type: Corporation / Trading Company

Founder: Founded by the merger of several Dutch trading companies under authorization from the Dutch government

Origin: Dutch Republic

Age/Duration: Approximately 200 years (1602–1799)

What value it generated: The Dutch East India Company had a massive global trading network. Their global capital allocation was unprecedented at the time. Their economical growth and wealth creation is still considered legendary. Their robust maritime logistics and transportation systems, commercial links between continents, new organizational and financial innovations were the reasons for their immense growth. They were one of the earliest large scale multinational corporations.

Why it appears to have failed: The Dutch East India Company appears to have failed because despite immense wealth and influence, it struggled with corruption, bureaucratic inefficiency, rising competition, military expenses, and financial mismanagement. While it generated enormous value for a period, it may have lacked the adaptability and institutional renewal necessary to survive changing conditions and hence coming to an end in the late 1700s or early 1800s.

Questions it raises:
1. Why do some highly successful systems collapse despite generating enormous value?
2. Can wealth compensate for weak governance?
3. Is corruption a natural failure mode of large institutions?
4. How important is institutional renewal for long-term survival?
5. What happens when the incentives of individuals diverge from the incentives of the institution?
6. Can a system become too successful for its own survival?

Possible Mechanisms: 
- Success Mechanisms
  - Resource generation
  - Scale
  - Financial innovation
  - Trade networks
- Failure Mechanisms
  - Corruption
  - Buereacratic inefficiency
  - Misaligned incentives
  - Inability to adapt
  - Financial mismanagement
  - institutional stagnation
