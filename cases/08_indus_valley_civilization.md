# Indus Valley Civilization

Type: Civilization

Founder: No known founder

Origin: Indus River Basin (Modern India and Pakistan)

Age/Duration: 2700 to 3100 years (c. 3900 BCE to 1200 BCE)

What value it generated: The Indus Valley Civilization is known for its large scale urban planning. Their urban planning included advanced drainage and sanitation systems which were unprecedented for its time. They had trade routes globally with their own, what is believed to be the world's first dock at Lothal. They possessed standardized weights and measures. They was considered one of the top most advanced human civilizations of its time.

Why it appears to have survived and suddenly failed: There are so many hypothesis on how the civilization came to an end. None are proven till date and it seems to be a combination of shifting river systems, environmental change or a plague like disease. Unlike other civilizations, its writing and records are regionally deciphered but not universally accepted which creates a huge gap in our understanding of its other advancements or innovations as well as its decline.
IMPORTANT: This is only a hypothesis. Historians still debate the causes.

Questions it raises:
1. What happens when cities survive but knowledge transmission fails?
2. Why did the Indus Valley leave relatively little recoverable written knowledge compared to Egypt?
3. Can a system survive without strong mechanisms for preserving identity?
4. What is the relationship between infrastructure and civilizational survival?
5. How dependent are enduring systems on stable resource generation?

Possible Mechanisms:
- Resource stability
- Environmental resilience
- Knowledge preservation
- Economic coordination
- Advanced infrastructure
- Unprecedented innovations
- Institutional continuity
