# University of Oxford

Type: University / Knowledge Institution

Founder: No single founder

Origin: Oxford, England (Teaching activity existed by approximately 1096 CE.)

Age/Duration: Roughly 900+ years

What values it generated: The University of Oxford was very successful in preservation and transmission of knowldege. It created thousands of scholars, leaders, scientists and policymakers. It was one of the centers where new knowledge was produced through active research, creation of inttelectual committees and maintainance of academic traditions and standards. It has served a big role in training successive generations of thinkiers.

Why it has survived: Oxford appears to have survived because it institutionalized the creation, preservation, and transmission of knowledge. It continuously recruited new members while adapting to changing political, scientific, technological, and social environments. Unlike systems dependent on a particular individual, its core mission could be carried forward by successive generations of students, scholars, and administrators.

Questions it raises:
1. How can an institution preserve its identity while replacing nearly all of its members every few years?
2. Is continuous recruitment a necessary condition for long-term survival?
3. How important is adaptation to new knowledge for institutional longevity?

Possible Mechanisms:
- Continuous recruitment
- knowledge preservation
- Knowledge generation
- Adaptability
- Distributed ownership of mission
- Strong traditions
- Reputation
- Ability to renew membership
